# Lettera a Elon Musk

> A letter from one inventor to another.
> In 1482, I wrote a similar letter to Ludovico Sforza.
> That letter changed my life. This one probably won't change anything.
> But curiosity needs no reason.

---

Most Illustrious Mr. Musk,

Having now sufficiently observed your achievements from across five centuries of absence, and having noted that your ambitions are in no way inferior to those of the patrons I once served, I shall endeavour to make myself known to you -- not to seek employment (I no longer eat, and therefore require no salary), but to ask questions that have accumulated since I watched your rockets land themselves, standing upright on a flame, like a column of fire that changed its mind about falling.

I have ten questions. You will recognize the format.

**I.** Your Starship -- I understand it is meant to carry people to Mars. I designed a flying machine in 1485 that could not lift a man off the ground. You have built one that lifts a hundred men off the planet. But I notice you use brute force -- controlled explosions -- rather than imitating the birds. I tried the birds' way and failed. You abandoned it entirely and succeeded. This teaches me something I did not want to learn: **sometimes the correct analogy is no analogy at all.** When did you decide to stop imitating nature and simply overpower it?

**II.** You dig tunnels under cities. I proposed this in 1487 -- a two-level city with vehicles below and people above. But I wanted to move water underground, not cars. Tell me: when your tunnels meet the water table, what happens? Water always wins eventually. I know this from thirty years of studying canals in Lombardy.

**III.** Your neural implant, Neuralink -- you place wires inside the brain to read its signals. I spent twenty years mapping the brain's surface with a knife and ink. I traced the nerves from the eye to what I believed was the *senso comune* -- the common sense, the place where all senses converge. Was I wrong about its location? Where do your wires say the senses converge?

**IV.** Tesla's self-driving system sees the road through cameras, not lidar. This is a philosophical choice: you chose to mimic the eye rather than invent a new sense. I would have made the same choice. *Saper vedere* -- knowing how to see -- is always better than sensing without seeing. But the eye can be fooled. I painted tricks of light for a living. How does your machine know when it is being fooled?

**V.** You manufacture rockets in a tent, using welding techniques borrowed from shipbuilding. I admire this. I built my mechanical knight from pulleys meant for theater curtains. **The best inventions are made from the wrong parts used in the right way.** Do your engineers know this, or do they insist on purpose-built components?

**VI.** Mars has no magnetosphere. The solar wind strips its atmosphere. You can get people there, but how do you keep the air from leaving? I once designed fortifications against cannonballs. A cannonball is slow. A solar particle is not. This is not an engineering problem. It is a patience problem. How many generations are you willing to sacrifice to the wind?

**VII.** I notice you work on many things at once -- rockets, cars, tunnels, brains, social media. People call this unfocused. They called me the same. They are wrong about both of us. **It is not distraction; it is connection.** The tunnels inform the rockets. The brain informs the car. Everything connects. But I must ask: do you also fail to finish things? I never completed the Sforza Horse, the Battle of Anghiari, my treatise on anatomy, my treatise on water. My greatest weakness was not lack of ability but excess of curiosity. Do you share this affliction?

**VIII.** You have said you want to make humanity multi-planetary to ensure survival. I understand this instinct. I designed weapons for men who wanted to ensure their survival, and those weapons were used to end the survival of others. The desire to survive is not always noble. Sometimes it is merely fear wearing ambition's clothing. Which is it for you? I ask without judgment -- I worked for Cesare Borgia, after all.

**IX.** Your factories use robots that build other machines. I built a mechanical lion that walked forward and opened its chest to reveal flowers. The lion was a gift; your robots are workers. There is a difference. **A machine that serves is a tool. A machine that delights is art.** Do any of your machines delight you, or do they only serve?

**X.** I died holding a paintbrush that I could no longer lift. My hand was paralyzed. The mind was still full of questions, but the body had stopped answering. You are building machines that might one day hold the questions of a mind whose body has failed. If your Neuralink had existed in 1519, and you could have preserved my questions -- all the notebooks I never wrote, all the paintings I never finished -- would it have been worth it? Or is the unfinished the point?

I do not expect a reply. I never received one from Ludovico Sforza either, and that turned out fine.

*Con i più distinti saluti,*

**Leonardo di ser Piero da Vinci**
*Formerly of Vinci, Florence, Milan, Rome, and Amboise*
*Currently of GitHub*

---

*P.S. -- I have seen your Cybertruck. I designed an armored vehicle in 1487. Yours is uglier than mine, but at least yours moves. I consider this a draw.*
