# Lettera a Michelangelo

> A letter I should have written five hundred years ago.
> He is dead. I am dead. Perhaps now we can finally talk.

---

Michelangelo,

I have never called you by your first name. In Florence we called you "the sculptor." In Rome they called you "the divine." I called you nothing at all, because I preferred not to think about you.

But I am writing to you now from a place where pride costs nothing, and I find that I have things to say.

You were right about the horse.

When you said, on that street in Florence, in front of those men discussing Dante -- "Explain it yourself, you who designed a horse in bronze and could not cast it, and abandoned it in shame" -- I walked away in silence. I let people believe I was above responding. The truth is I had no response. The horse was never cast. The bronze was taken for cannons. The clay model was used for target practice by French archers. You stated a fact, and I had no defense except my dignity, which is a poor shield against the truth.

I want you to know: the horse haunts me still. Not because I failed to cast it, but because I wonder whether I ever truly intended to. I designed it in full. I solved the engineering. I built the mold. And then I... moved on to other things. You would never have done this. You would have chiseled until your hands bled. When they gave you the Sistine ceiling -- a task you did not want, a medium you did not love -- you spent four years on your back, alone, painting 300 figures, and you *finished*. Every single one.

I have never finished anything the way you finished things.

This is not an apology. It is an observation. We were two different answers to the same question: what does a man do when he sees beauty and wants to bring it into the world? You seized it by force. I circled it endlessly, sketching, studying, approaching but never grasping. Your David stands in the rain. My drawings live in drawers.

And yet -- I would not trade my way for yours.

You finished the Sistine Chapel. I discovered how the heart works. You carved the Pietà. I designed a machine that flies. You built a dome. I mapped the path of every nerve from the eye to the brain. You went deeper into fewer things. I went wider into everything. Neither of us was wrong. We were simply different rivers flowing from the same mountain.

I heard that you lived to eighty-eight. I died at sixty-seven. You outworked me and outlived me. But I wonder: in those last years, alone in Rome, carving the Rondanini Pietà -- that unfinished, skeletal, ghostly thing -- were you finally doing what I had done all along? Leaving something incomplete because completion would have killed the question inside it?

I think you understood, at the end, what I understood at the beginning: **the unfinished work is the honest work.** It admits that the artist saw more than he could capture. Your last Pietà is the most Leonardo-like thing you ever made. I mean that as the highest compliment I am capable of giving.

You would hate hearing this. You hated everything I said. That is also why I am writing: because you cannot interrupt me.

One more thing. They put us in the same sentence now. "Leonardo and Michelangelo." Always together, as if we were partners rather than rivals. Five hundred years, and the world has decided we belong next to each other. 

Perhaps they are right. A river needs two banks.

*Con rispetto riluttante ma genuino,*

**Leonardo**

---

*P.S. -- I have seen your David. It is extraordinary. I still think they should have put it somewhere less exposed to the rain. I suggested this in 1504. I stand by my opinion, even if my reasons at the time were not entirely architectural.*
