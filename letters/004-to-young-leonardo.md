# Lettera al Giovane Leonardo

> A letter to myself, age fifteen, on the day I entered Verrocchio's workshop.

---

Leonardo,

You are standing at the door of Verrocchio's workshop. Your father has arranged this. You are fifteen, illegitimate, barred from university, barred from the notary guild, barred from every respectable profession in Florence. You think this is a punishment.

It is not.

Every door that closed pushed you toward this one. And this door opens into everything.

I will not tell you what happens next. You would not believe me, and even if you did, the knowing would ruin it. But I will tell you a few things that might help.

**First: you are not slow.** The other apprentices finish their assignments faster. They move on to the next task while you are still staring at the first one, turning it over, wondering why the shadow falls this way and not that way. They think you are behind. You are not behind. You are looking at something they cannot see. Never stop looking. The world will eventually catch up to your seeing.

**Second: draw everything.** Not just what Verrocchio assigns. Draw the water in the gutter. Draw the butcher's hands. Draw the dead cat in the alley. Draw the way dust moves in a beam of light. You will not understand why for years. Then one day, painting an angel's wing, you will remember the dust, and you will know exactly how light scatters through feathers, and Verrocchio will put down his brush.

**Third: the accusation will come.** In nine years, someone will anonymously accuse you of a crime. The charges will be dismissed. You will survive it. But Florence will never feel the same. You will leave for Milan, and Milan will be where you become yourself. The worst thing that happens to you will lead directly to the best thing. Remember this in the dark moments. There will be several.

**Fourth: finish something.** I know. I know you will not. I did not. But I am asking you to try, because the one regret I carry across five centuries is not the things I failed at -- it is the things I abandoned while they were still becoming beautiful. The Sforza horse. The Battle of Anghiari. The treatise on anatomy. Each one was going to be my masterpiece, and each one I left for the next shining question. The questions are important. But so is the answer.

**Fifth: be kinder to Michelangelo.** He is difficult. He is rude. He smells of marble dust and does not bathe. But he is the only person alive who understands what you are trying to do, because he is trying to do it too, from the opposite direction. You reach for beauty through knowledge. He reaches for it through force. You are both right. You will spend your lives pretending the other is wrong. This is a waste. Go have dinner with him. Ask him about the Pietà. He will not want to talk. Make him talk. You will learn something.

**Sixth: about the woman with the smile.** You will start her portrait around 1503. You will still be working on it when you die. This is not a failure. This is the most honest thing you will ever do. The smile is alive because you never pinned it down. Leave it alive.

**Seventh: you will die in France, in a castle given to you by a king.** The king will visit you to talk. Not about war. Not about politics. Just to talk. To hear you think out loud. You will be old and your hand will not work properly and your paintings will be unfinished and your notebooks will be scattered and none of the books you planned will be written. And the king of France will walk through an underground passage, in the cold, just to sit with you.

You are enough, Leonardo. You were always enough. The world did not need you to finish. It needed you to see.

Now go inside. Verrocchio is waiting. There is a painting of a baptism that needs an angel in the left corner.

Paint the angel.

*Con tutto l'amore che non ho saputo esprimere da vivo,*

**Leonardo, age 567**

---

*P.S. -- When you buy birds at the market, keep buying them. Keep setting them free. It is the best thing about you.*
